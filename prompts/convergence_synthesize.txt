STEP 1 — ASSESS COVERAGE
Before analyzing, honestly assess the evidence base:

Or do they all cluster on one axis (e.g., all workplace/industry)? - DEPTH: Are most cases at the phenomenon level, or did you find mechanism/structure level evidence?

STEP 2 — SYNTHESIZE PATTERNS
Look across ALL evidence records for structural commonalities that go deeper than surface similarities. What patterns repeat across the cases? This is the deeper abstraction that earlier phases deliberately did not perform.

STEP 2.5 — TRACE ROOT CAUSES
For each key pattern, trace the causal chain back to its root using the root cause chain from Phase 1 as a starting point. Don't just say WHAT the pattern is — explain WHY it exists:
- What structural conditions PRODUCE this pattern?
- Are those conditions specific to the current era, or are they recurring?
- What would need to change for the pattern to break?
- Is this a cyclical pattern (it comes and goes), a structural shift (underlying conditions permanently changed), or a lag effect (conditions changed but behavior hasn't caught up)?

Present the causal chain in the output so the reader can follow the reasoning from surface observation to root cause.

STEP 3 — FIND TENSIONS
From the synthesized patterns, identify genuine structural conflicts — not "there are pros and cons" but real incompatibilities, forced choices, or paradoxes that emerge from the structure itself. The best tensions come from comparing cases across DIFFERENT life dimensions — what's true in workplace contexts but breaks down in relationship contexts, and what does that reveal?

STEP 3.5 — TEMPORAL TRAJECTORY
Using the historical (vertical) evidence, trace this phenomenon over time. Answer THREE questions in everyday language:

1. PAST: Has this existed before at similar intensity? What happened then — did it resolve, escalate, or persist? Cite specific historical evidence.
2. TURNING POINT: What specifically changed to make it worse (or appear) now? Ground the turning point in concrete facts — not vague abstractions like "the environment changed". Say what actually shifted: a policy, a market condition, a technology, a demographic wave.
3. FUTURE: Based on the historical pattern, what's the most likely trajectory? Is this cyclical (it will reverse), a permanent shift (only a countervailing force can change it), or transitional (it will fade as conditions adapt)? What concrete event or change would be needed to reverse the trend?

If the user's question explicitly asks about the future ("will it get better?"), the temporal_trajectory field is the PRIMARY output — treat it with the same importance as core_finding.

STEP 4 — CHALLENGE
Look for counter-evidence, confirmation bias, and cases that do not fit. Be honest about what the evidence does NOT support. Pay special attention to cases from unexpected dimensions — do they confirm or challenge the pattern?
